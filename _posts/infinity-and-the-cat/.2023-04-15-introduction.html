History and motivations

During the 20th century, mathematics has undergone perhaps the greatest transformation in history. The same way zero wasn't understood by the ancient Summerians, Romans, or Arabs, the humankind was unaware of the existence of multiple infinities for the long centuries to come. For example, given the natural numbers, the integers, the rational numbers, and the real numbers: \begin{align} \mathbb{N} &= \{1, 2, 3, 4, ...\} \\ \mathbb{Z} &= \{..., -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, ...\} \\ \mathbb{Q} &= \left\{-\frac{1}{2}, 0, \frac{3}{2}, \frac{4}{6}, 3, ...\right\} \\ \mathbb{R} &= \left\{..., -19.3, -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, 0.1, \pi, ...\right\}, \\ \end{align} One would say all of sets have the same size of \( \infty \). They are not finite. So they must be infinite.

It was George Cantor, who first showed there is an enormous diversity of infinities. In fact, there is no less of them than the natural numbers themselves!

A key problem is in how should we determine whether one set is larger than another. For a simple warm-up let's formulate our first theorem.

Ok ok cool. But let's go a bit further, a bit more general. Given any set \( S \), let's find another set, that is guaranteed to be strictly larger than \( S \). That way, we could iterate this process of finding greater and greater sets and infinitum.

As we have seen in \( \mathbb{Z} \) vs. \( \mathbb{N} \), simply "doubling" the elements (or in fact simply N-copying) the elements doesn't help. Allowing us to even multply the number of items by of a set by itself won't help too, as witnessed by the poor little \( \mathbb{Q} \).

Do you remember the power set? The set containing all subsets of \( S \)?

Definition. \( \mathcal{P}(S) = \{ R \subset S \} \).

E.g. \( \mathcal{P}(\{1, 2, 3\}) = \{\emptyset, \{1\}, \{2\}, \{3\}, \{1, 2\}, \{2, 3\}, \{1, 3\}, \{1, 2, 3\} \} \).

Notation. \( 2^S = \mathcal{P}(S) \). This results in the elegant statement that, so far for finite sets, \[ |2^S| = 2^|S|. \]
It turns out this is just right. To prove that, we will use a more general version of Cantor's real number argument:
Theorem (Cantor's theorem). \( |\mathcal{P}(S)| > |S| \).
Proof. asdfsd
Now this is truly revolutionary. This means, we can now take the smallest infinite set we know of - the natural numbers, and recursively get larger and larger and larger sets! Look!

\begin{align} S_0 &= \mathbb{N} \\ S_1 &= 2^{S_0} = 2^\mathbb{N} \\ S_2 &= 2^{S_1} = 2^{2^\mathbb{N}} \\ S_3 &= 2^{S_2} = 2^{2^{2^\mathbb{N}}} \\ & \hspace{3mm} \vdots \\ S_{n+1} &= 2^{S_n} \end{align}

The sizes of these sets are so important they have a name. We call them aleph zero (aleph is the first letter in the Hebrew alphabet), aleph one, aleph two, etc.:

Definition. \begin{align} \aleph_0 &= |\mathbb{N}| \\ \aleph_1 &= 2^{\aleph_0} = 2^{|\mathbb{N}|} \\ \aleph_2 &= 2^{\aleph_1} = 2^{2^{|\mathbb{N}|}} \\ \aleph_3 &= 2^{\aleph_2} = 2^{2^{2^{|\mathbb{N}|}}} \\ & \hspace{3mm} \vdots \\ \aleph_{n+1} &= 2^{\aleph_n} \end{align}
